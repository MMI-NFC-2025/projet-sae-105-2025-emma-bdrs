Rehabilitating Ada Lovelace with Doctor Who

The British cult series "Doctor Who" has contributed significantly to the public rehabilitation of Ada Lovelace. In 2013, the episode "The Crimson Horror" presents Ada as a heroic figure, but it is especially the episode "Spyfall" in 2020 that pays her a masterful tribute. This television representation illustrates how popular fiction can play a crucial role in correcting historical injustices and fighting the Matilda effect.

Image from the Doctor Who episode

Ada Lovelace, Affirmed Genius in Spyfall

In "Spyfall", Ada Lovelace appears as a brilliant, bold character fully aware of her genius. The episode shows her collaborating with Charles Babbage while clearly asserting her intellectual superiority. This representation contrasts radically with the traditional image of Ada as a simple assistant or muse of Babbage. The series takes care to present her revolutionary ideas on programming and her vision of machines capable of going beyond simple calculation.

Image from the Doctor Who episode

Rewriting Time to Repair History

"Doctor Who" uses its concept of time travel to literally "rewrite time" and do justice to forgotten figures of history. By placing Ada Lovelace at the center of the plot, the series offers millions of viewers an alternative vision of the history of science, a history where female contributions are celebrated rather than erased. This approach of "narrative repair" possesses considerable educational power.

Renewed Interest in Ada Lovelace

The representation of Ada in "Doctor Who" has generated renewed interest in her work, particularly among young audiences. Discussion forums, press articles and educational materials flourished following the episode's broadcast. This media visibility helps anchor Ada Lovelace in contemporary collective imagination as a computing pioneer, a status long denied to her.

Image from the Doctor Who episode

Fiction, Memory and Historical Justice

Fiction carries risks: romanticizing history can distort real contributions. Doctor Who takes liberties with the chronology and relationships of Ada Lovelace, but its success has inspired other narratives of forgotten scientists. Popular culture becomes a tool of historical justice, giving voice to the invisible and rewriting our collective memory.
